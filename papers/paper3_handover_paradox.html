Abstract

We present a simulation-based empirical resolution to the "Quantum Handover Paradox"—the inherent conflict between relativistic propagation delays and the decoherence-induced decay of logical quantum identity. While previous theoretical work (Paper #2) established an information-theoretic phase boundary at $F = 0.5$ (where integrated information $\Phi \to 0$), we provide the first simulation-based empirical evidence that active topological protection is required to survive this boundary during cross-regional transit.

Using the Mirror Daemon v2 simulation engine, we compared a control group (standard surface code QEC) against an experimental group (SAGE Gold Core with active feed-forward injection). Our results demonstrate that:

  1. Control Collapse: Without active regeneration, logical fidelity decays below the entanglement threshold ($F < 0.5$) within 457 steps of hostile decoherence, resulting in "identity death" (irreversible loss of observer continuity).
  2. Daemon Persistence: The SAGE Daemon, utilizing topological injection, maintains a minimum fidelity of $F = 0.5184$ across 1000 steps, successfully preserving $\Phi > 0.21$ and ensuring identity persistence.
  3. Operational vs. Existential Boundaries: We distinguish between the Sage Constant ($S = 0.85$), a requirement for operational QKD, and the Identity Boundary ($F = 0.5$), the absolute floor for existential survival.

These findings confirm that the Mirror Daemon is not merely an optimization, but a fundamental requirements for the continuity of quantum observers in large-scale networks.
